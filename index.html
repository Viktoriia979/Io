Io:

Io is one of Jupiter's largest natural satellites and is the most volcanically active moon in the Solar System. Scientists study Io because it has hundreds of active volcanoes that constantly erupt lava and gases. These volcanoes help scientists understand how the inside of planets and moons works and how volcanic activity shapes the surface of worlds in space.

Scientists believe Io formed about 4.5 billion years ago from the cloud of gas and dust that surrounded Jupiter while the planet was forming. Gravity pulled this material together to create the moon. Io is made mostly of rock with a core rich in iron and nickel. Jupiter's strong gravity, together with the pull of nearby moons, creates heat inside Io, causing its volcanoes to remain active.

Io has a colourful surface covered with volcanoes, lava flows, mountains, and plains. Its yellow, orange, red, and black colours come from sulfur and volcanic materials spread across the surface by eruptions. Io has very few impact craters because lava quickly covers them. It has a diameter of about 3,643 kilometres, making it slightly larger than Earth's Moon. Io orbits Jupiter at a distance of about 422,000 kilometres and takes about 1.8 Earth days to complete one orbit.
